About Me

I'm Matt Brems and I currently teach data science with General Assembly. I'm afforded the great opportunity and responsibility to train data scientists across the United States.

Why am I a data science instructor?

  • I firmly believe that our world is made better when decisions are informed by data.
  • Data science is made better by properly relying on rigorous statistical methods.
  • Rigorous statistical methods are often inaccessible without serious academic training in mathematics.
  • Our world does not reach its full potential if we limit data-driven decision-making to those with serious academic training.

I'm particularly passionate about bridging the gap between theoretical statistics and real-world insights. I want to contribute my voice to the chorus of individuals who work tirelessly to make data-driven decision-making accessible, sexy, and fun - to all people, not just those who enjoy calculus or who know what a maximum likelihood estimator is.

Previously, I did political data work for 0ptimus Consulting, earned my M.S. in statistics from Ohio State, and studied math, economics and political science at Franklin College.

Beyond General Assembly, I spend my time buying and partially reading statistics textbooks, overcaffeinating, re-watching TV shows, or ordering unhealthy delivery food with my boyfriend and our pug.

Research

While I have worked in statistical genetics and have dabbled in cryptography and discrete topology, I invest most of my time in identifying ways to be a better educator. I believe quantitative literacy and statistical savvy are two underrated skills and that society's needs here exceed the current state of statistical education.

Whether it's articulating the process for principal component analysis or discussing how thinking probabilistically is hard but important, I attempt to identify important topics and find a meaningful way to communicate these topics to people with various technical backgronds.

To this end, I've written a number of posts. I'm probably proudest of the following:

  • "A One-Stop Shop for Principal Component Analysis," a longer post designed to thoroughly yet gently explain PCA.
  • "People are talking about 'reproducibility crises' and 'p-values' and I feel like I should understand but my eyes are already glazing over… can you help?," a post that aims to intuitively communicate what the reproducibility crisis is, why it's a big deal, how p-values might be part of the problem, and how we can attempt to fix the issue.
  • "The New Hot Take: Machine Learning is Racist," a post describing how algorithms aren't, by themselves, racist - they have help.
